Abstract

Rapid habitat fragmentation and escalating human-wildlife conflict have contributed to steep global declines in cheetah (Acinonyx jubatus) populations, currently estimated at approximately 7,500 individuals worldwide. South Africa holds roughly 1,000 of these individuals and represents the only national population currently exhibiting net growth. This study analyzes how the landscape of the Limpopo province in northern South Africa can be leveraged to support expanded cheetah populations using three complementary approaches: NDVI reclassification of satellite-derived remote sensing data; multi-criteria analysis (MCDA) of tracking methods; and effective population size estimation to contextualize minimum viable population requirements. Approximately 72% of the free-range study area falls within vegetation classes broadly suitable for cheetah occupancy. MVP analysis yielded Ne ≈ 984, approaching the revised minimum threshold of 1,000.
